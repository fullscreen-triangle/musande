\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We isolate a single distinction that, once made, dissolves a family of
apparent paradoxes about time, entropy, and reversibility: the distinction
between \emph{occupying} a state and \emph{specifying} a state. Occupation is
what a dynamical system does---it is at a microstate because its flow took it
there. Specification is what an embedded agent does---it singles out, prepares,
or recognises a state by drawing a boundary that individuates it from
everything else. We prove that these are governed by opposite economies.
Specification of a low-probability configuration is irreducibly costly: it
requires importing order, depositing a positive boundary residue, and (when
the configuration is held fixed in advance) erasing the information that fixes
it, at a cost no less than the entropy the configuration appears to save.
Occupation of the same configuration is, by contrast, free: a bounded
measure-preserving flow visits every accessible region, including
low-probability ones, with no agent, no preparation, and no cost---only with
\emph{low frequency}. We then show that the standard mental moves by which one
tries to make an improbable state ``reachable cheaply''---renaming a
later state the \emph{initial} state, calling a \emph{specific} state an
\emph{arranged} state, or imagining a configuration in which the low-entropy
state is the \emph{most probable} next step---are each a single
category error: they transfer a property true of \emph{specification} onto
\emph{occupation}, or they redefine the one property (low probability) that
constitutes the state. As a worked application we treat the Loschmidt
reversal. We prove that the reversed configuration is not the initial
configuration (an exact result about identity), that the prepared reversal
pays for itself (an exact result about specification cost), and that the
spontaneous reversal is neither incoherent nor arranged but merely
\emph{atypical} (an exact result about occupation frequency). The apparent
incoherence of the reversal is shown to be the felt difficulty of
\emph{specifying} it, mistaken for an impossibility of its \emph{occurring}.
The development is structural; no claim depends on any particular physical
system, and where a tempting stronger claim could not be supported it is
marked as out of reach rather than asserted.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} occupation versus specification; boundary residue;
individuation; low-probability states; Loschmidt's paradox; statistical arrow
of time; Poincar\'e recurrence; Landauer cost; typicality; category error.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{The distinction}

A bounded dynamical system is, at every moment, in exactly one microstate.
That microstate is maximally specific: it is one point out of an astronomically
large state space, and the system arrived there for no reason other than that
its flow took it there. No agent chose it; no construction produced it; it is
simply where the system \emph{is}. We call this relation \emph{occupation}: a
system \emph{occupies} a state.

An embedded agent that studies the system performs a different act. To speak of
a particular configuration---to prepare it, to recognise it, to single it out
among its alternatives---the agent must \emph{individuate} the configuration:
mark it off from everything it is not. We call this relation
\emph{specification}: an agent \emph{specifies} a state.

This paper's single thesis is that occupation and specification obey
\emph{opposite economies}, and that conflating them is the source of a
recurring class of confusions about entropy and the direction of time.

\begin{principle}[Occupation--Specification Asymmetry]
\label{prin:asym}
Let $X$ be a configuration of low probability (few microstates relative to its
alternatives). Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.4em,nosep]
\item \emph{Specification of $X$ is costly}: an embedded agent cannot single out,
prepare, or recognise $X$ without an irreducible expenditure---boundary residue,
imported order, or erased information---bounded below by a positive constant and,
in the prepared case, no less than the entropy $X$ appears to save.
\item \emph{Occupation of $X$ is free}: a bounded measure-preserving flow visits
$X$ with no agent and no cost, differing from its visits to high-probability
configurations only in \emph{frequency}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{principle}

The asymmetry is intuitive once stated: it is hard to \emph{find} a needle in a
haystack, but the needle is in the haystack whether or not anyone looks, and the
haystack contains, at every instant, some specific---and individually
improbable---arrangement of straws that no one assembled.

\subsection{Why the distinction matters}

Many arguments that purport to show low-entropy states are impossible,
incoherent, or self-contradictory turn out, on inspection, to establish only
that such states are \emph{costly to specify}---and then to transfer that
conclusion, illegitimately, to the claim that they are \emph{impossible to
occur}. The transfer is a category error: it moves a property of the agent's
relation to the state (specification) onto the system's relation to the state
(occupation). \Cref{prin:asym} blocks the transfer at its root.

We make the principle precise (\Cref{sec:framework}), prove the two halves
(\Cref{sec:spec,sec:occ}), classify the category errors that violate it
(\Cref{sec:errors}), and apply the whole to the Loschmidt reversal
(\Cref{sec:loschmidt}), where the felt incoherence of the reversal is shown to
be exactly the cost of specifying it, mistaken for an impossibility of its
occurring.

\subsection{Method and scope}

The argument is structural. We assume only a bounded, measure-preserving,
finitely resolvable dynamical setting (\Cref{sec:framework}); the two economies
follow from this alone. We borrow three standard results---Poincar\'e
recurrence \citep{poincare1890}, the Liouville theorem \citep{arnold1989}, and
the Landauer bound on information erasure
\citep{landauer1961,bennett1982}---and one prior structural result of the
author's, the positivity of the boundary residue (the resolution floor) for an
embedded individuating agent. Where a stronger claim (e.g.\ that the reversal is
\emph{logically} incoherent rather than \emph{statistically} atypical) could not
be supported, it is marked as out of reach.

\section{Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{States, macrostates, probability}

\begin{definition}[Bounded measure space]
\label{def:space}
We work on a bounded phase space $\Space$ carrying a finite measure $\mu$ with
$0<\mu(\Space)<\infty$, and a measure-preserving flow $\phi_t:\Space\to\Space$.
A \emph{microstate} is a point $x\in\Space$. A \emph{macrostate} is a
positive-measure measurable set $\Wmac\subseteq\Space$; its \emph{probability}
is $p(\Wmac)=\mu(\Wmac)/\mu(\Space)$, and its \emph{entropy} (up to the
Boltzmann constant) is $S(\Wmac)=\kB\log\mu(\Wmac)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Entropy \emph{is} log-probability]
\label{rem:entropy-is-prob}
$S(\Wmac)$ and $\log p(\Wmac)$ differ only by the additive constant
$-\kB\log\mu(\Space)$. ``Low entropy'' and ``low probability'' are therefore not
two correlated properties of a macrostate but \emph{one property under two
names}. This identity is used repeatedly below; in particular, no operation can
raise a macrostate's probability while holding its entropy fixed, because the
two move together by definition. We return to this in \Cref{sec:errors}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Occupation and specification}

\begin{definition}[Occupation]
\label{def:occ}
A system \emph{occupies} microstate $x$ at time $t$ if $\phi_t(x_0)=x$ for its
initial condition $x_0$. Occupation is a relation between the flow and a point;
it involves no agent and no choice.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Specification]
\label{def:spec}
An \emph{embedded agent} is a subsystem of $\Space$ with finite descriptive
resources. The agent \emph{specifies} a configuration $X$ when it individuates
$X$: draws a separator marking $X$ off from $\Space\setminus X$ to the agent's
finite resolution. Three modes of specification are distinguished:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(S\arabic*)},leftmargin=3em,nosep]
\item\label{s:point} \emph{Pointing}: singling out $X$ from its alternatives
(naming, recognising).
\item\label{s:prepare} \emph{Preparing}: forcing the system into $X$ by acting
on it (setting coordinates, reversing momenta).
\item\label{s:recognise} \emph{Recognising}: certifying, after the fact, that
the system is in $X$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[The two relations are categorically distinct]
\label{rem:two-relations}
Occupation relates the flow to a point and needs no mind. Specification relates
an agent to a region and is an act with resources and a cost. A system occupies
a maximally specific microstate at every instant without any agent specifying it;
conversely, an agent may specify a region the system never occupies. The
remainder of the paper is the consequence of keeping these apart.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The boundary residue}

We take as given the following structural result, established for an embedded
finitely-resolved individuating agent (the resolution-floor theorem of the
companion development): every separator an embedded agent can realise is a union
of positive-measure cells and therefore carries measure bounded below by a
strictly positive constant.

\begin{theorem}[Positivity of the boundary residue]
\label{thm:floor}
Let an embedded agent of finite resolution individuate a region $X$ from
$\Space\setminus X$ within a connected bounded $\Space$. Then the agent's
realisable separator $\Sigma(X)$ has measure
$\mu(\Sigma(X))\ge\floorc>0$, where $\floorc$ is the agent's resolution floor.
No individuation an embedded agent performs is free.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Reference]
The realisable separator is a union of cells of the agent's finite partition;
by connectedness it contains at least one cell, and each cell has measure
$\ge\mu_{\min}>0$; hence $\mu(\Sigma(X))\ge\mu_{\min}=:\floorc$. The full
development, including the necessity of boundedness, appears in the companion
paper on the irreducible boundary of a bounded resolvable space.
\end{proof}

% =====================================================================
\section{The economy of specification}
\label{sec:spec}
% =====================================================================

We prove that each mode of specification of a low-probability configuration is
costly, establishing \Cref{prin:asym}(i).

\subsection{Pointing and recognising are costly}

\begin{proposition}[Pointing cost]
\label{prop:point-cost}
For an embedded agent, singling out a configuration $X$ (mode~\ref{s:point})
deposits boundary residue $\ge\floorc>0$, independently of $X$'s probability. If
$X$ is low-probability, the cost of \emph{locating} $X$ among its alternatives
grows with the improbability: to point to one macrostate among $K$ equiprobable
alternatives requires resolving $\log K$ bits of distinction, each carrying
residue $\ge\floorc$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Singling out $X$ is an individuation; by \Cref{thm:floor} it carries residue
$\ge\floorc$. Discriminating $X$ from $K-1$ alternatives is a sequence of
$\lceil\log_2 K\rceil$ binary individuations, each contributing residue
$\ge\floorc$; the total is $\ge\floorc\lceil\log_2 K\rceil$. For a
low-probability $X$, $K$ is large and the locating cost is correspondingly
large.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Recognition cost]
\label{cor:recog-cost}
Recognising (mode~\ref{s:recognise}) that the system occupies $X$ is an
individuation of $X$ against $\Space\setminus X$ and carries residue
$\ge\floorc$. There is no cost-free recognition.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Preparing a held-fixed low-probability state pays its own apparent saving}

The sharpest mode is preparation (mode~\ref{s:prepare}): forcing the system into
a low-probability $X$ chosen in advance. Here the cost is not merely positive but
is bounded below by the entropy the configuration appears to save.

\begin{theorem}[Preparation pays for itself]
\label{thm:landauer}
Let an embedded agent prepare the system into a pre-specified low-probability
macrostate $X$ by acting on it conditionally on knowledge of the system's prior
microstate. Let $\Delta S_X=S(\Wmac_{\mathrm{typ}})-S(X)>0$ be the entropy
deficit of $X$ relative to a typical macrostate. Then the agent's operation
increases the entropy of its environment by at least $\Delta S_X$:
\[
\Delta S_{\mathrm{env}}\ \ge\ \Delta S_X .
\]
The preparation produces no net entropy decrease.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Conditioning the action on the prior microstate requires the agent to acquire
and hold the information that distinguishes the target $X$ from its alternatives,
an amount $I_X\ge\Delta S_X/\kB$ bits (the deficit is, by
\Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob}, the log-ratio of macrostate measures). To reuse its
finite memory the agent must erase this information; by the Landauer bound
\citep{landauer1961,bennett1982}, erasing $I_X$ bits dissipates at least
$\kB T\ln 2\cdot I_X\ge T\,\Delta S_X$ of heat, raising environmental entropy by
$\ge\Delta S_X$. Hence the apparent saving $\Delta S_X$ inside $X$ is matched or
exceeded by $\Delta S_{\mathrm{env}}$; the books close.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What \Cref{thm:landauer} does and does not establish]
\label{rem:landauer-scope}
\Cref{thm:landauer} resolves the \emph{prepared} case completely: an agent that
arranges a low-probability configuration pays for it, and no second law is
violated. It says nothing about configurations that arise \emph{without}
preparation. That is the subject of the next section, and the whole weight of
the occupation--specification distinction rests on the gap between the two.
\end{remark}

% =====================================================================
\section{The economy of occupation}
\label{sec:occ}
% =====================================================================

We prove that occupation of a low-probability configuration is free and that the
only thing low about it is its frequency, establishing \Cref{prin:asym}(ii).

\subsection{Occupation requires no agent}

\begin{proposition}[Occupation is agent-free]
\label{prop:occ-free}
At every instant the system occupies a single, maximally specific microstate; no
agent specifies it, prepares it, or recognises it for the occupation to obtain.
In particular the current microstate of any system is, among the microstates of
its macrostate, an individually improbable point that no agent arranged.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Occupation is defined (\Cref{def:occ}) as $\phi_t(x_0)=x$: a fact about the flow,
not about any agent. The current microstate is one point of a positive-measure
macrostate containing astronomically many microstates; its individual
probability is the reciprocal of that count, hence small; and it was produced by
the flow, not by any specification (\Cref{rem:two-relations}). Thus improbable,
maximally specific, unarranged occupation is the generic condition of every
system at every instant.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Specificity does not entail arrangement]
\label{cor:spec-not-arr}
That a configuration is highly specific (low-probability, fine-tuned) does not
entail that any agent arranged it. The current microstate is a counterexample:
maximally specific, arranged by no one.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Low-probability configurations are occupied, freely, but rarely}

\begin{theorem}[Free but rare occupation]
\label{thm:rare}
Let $X\subseteq\Space$ be a low-probability macrostate, $p(X)>0$. Under the
bounded measure-preserving flow:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.4em,nosep]
\item \emph{Existence}: by Poincar\'e recurrence \citep{poincare1890}, almost
every orbit enters $X$ infinitely often; $X$ is occupied, with no agent and no
preparation.
\item \emph{Cost}: the occupation costs nothing---no work is done, no information
is erased, no order is imported---because no agent acts; the flow merely passes
through $X$.
\item \emph{Frequency}: by the ergodic average, the long-run fraction of time
spent in $X$ equals $p(X)=\mu(X)/\mu(\Space)$, which is small precisely because
$X$ is low-probability.
\end{enumerate}
Hence a low-probability configuration is occupied freely and spontaneously, and
the only respect in which it is ``hard to reach'' by the flow is that the flow
reaches it \emph{rarely}, not that reaching it is costly or forbidden.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i) is Poincar\'e recurrence on a finite-measure space. (ii) holds because
occupation is agent-free (\Cref{prop:occ-free}); there is no actor to incur a
cost. (iii) is the Birkhoff ergodic average for the indicator of $X$, equal to
$p(X)$ for an ergodic flow (and bounded by it in general by the recurrence
fraction). Smallness of the fraction is smallness of $\mu(X)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Symmetry of arrival cost]
\label{thm:arrival-symmetry}
Reaching a low-probability macrostate by the flow costs exactly as much as
reaching a high-probability one: nothing. If arrival at a typical macrostate
requires no agent and no work (it does not), then arrival at an atypical
macrostate requires no agent and no work either.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Both arrivals are occupation events (\Cref{def:occ}); by \Cref{prop:occ-free}
neither involves an agent. The flow's reaching $X$ is the same kind of event as
its reaching any region---an application of $\phi_t$. The configurations differ
in measure, not in the mechanism or cost of arrival. (Under a time-reversal
symmetry of $\phi_t$, the point is sharper still: the reflected orbit reaching
the reflected region is an equally valid, equally costless solution; what is not
symmetric is the \emph{measure} of the regions, never the cost of arrival.)
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The bedrock fact: rarity does not convert]
\label{rem:bedrock}
\Cref{thm:rare,thm:arrival-symmetry} together isolate the one irreducible fact.
A low-probability configuration is real (it is occupied---\ref{thm:rare}(i)),
uncompensated (no agent pays---\ref{thm:rare}(ii)), unarranged
(\Cref{cor:spec-not-arr}), and \emph{rare} (\ref{thm:rare}(iii)). Its rarity is
its identity: it cannot be made common without ceasing to be low-probability
(\Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob}), made impossible without contradicting recurrence,
or made arranged without an agent and a cost (\Cref{thm:landauer}). Rarity is
the residue that survives every attempted reframing.
\end{remark}

% =====================================================================
\section{The category errors}
\label{sec:errors}
% =====================================================================

We now classify the moves by which one attempts to violate \Cref{prin:asym}:
each transfers a specification-property onto occupation, or redefines the
constitutive property of the state. We state three; they exhaust the moves
encountered in the analysis of the Loschmidt reversal (\Cref{sec:loschmidt}).

\subsection{Error A: renaming a later state ``initial''}

\begin{proposition}[Naming does not confer no-past-ness]
\label{prop:err-rename}
Let a state $y$ be reached by the flow at time $t>0$, so $y$ has a past. Calling
$y$ ``the initial state'' is a labelling (mode~\ref{s:point}) and does not endow
$y$ with the property---having no past---that distinguishes an origin. Any
theorem whose hypothesis is ``has no past'' fails to apply to $y$ regardless of
$y$'s name.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
``Initial'' admits two senses: (a) a bookkeeping start chosen by the agent, and
(b) the structural origin, the unique state with no past. A reached state $y$ has
a past by construction ($y=\phi_t(x_0)$, $t>0$), so $y$ satisfies (a) by
stipulation and fails (b) by structure. A theorem proved for sense (b) reads the
predicate ``no past,'' which $y$ lacks; the rename supplies sense (a), which is
inert with respect to that predicate. Hence the rename transfers nothing.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Error B: inferring arrangement from specificity}

\begin{proposition}[Specific $\nRightarrow$ arranged]
\label{prop:err-arrange}
From ``$X$ is highly specific (low-probability)'' one cannot infer ``an agent
arranged $X$.'' The inference confuses a property of the outcome (its measure)
with a claim about its causal origin (an agent's action).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\Cref{cor:spec-not-arr} exhibits a standing counterexample: the system's current
microstate is maximally specific and arranged by no one. Specificity is measure;
arrangement is agency; the first does not imply the second. (Equivalently: a fair
process can yield an arbitrarily specific outcome with no intervention; the
outcome's improbability is evidence of its rarity, not of an arranger.)
\end{proof}

\subsection{Error C: making the low-probability state ``most probable''}

\begin{proposition}[One cannot raise probability at fixed entropy]
\label{prop:err-prob}
There is no operation that makes a low-entropy macrostate the most probable next
state while it remains low-entropy. ``Most probable'' and ``high entropy'' are
the same predicate (\Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob}); demanding a low-entropy state be
most probable demands a state be simultaneously least- and most-populated.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob}, $S(\Wmac)$ and $\log p(\Wmac)$ differ by a
constant; maximising $p$ maximises $S$. A state that is ``most probable next''
is, by definition, the largest accessible macrostate, i.e.\ the highest-entropy
one. To hold $S(X)$ low while making $p(X)$ maximal is to require
$\mu(X)=\min$ and $\mu(X)=\max$ simultaneously---a contradiction. Any apparent
construction that achieves it has silently enlarged $X$ (raised $\mu(X)$), i.e.\
changed the state to a different, higher-entropy one; the original low-entropy
state has not been made probable, it has been replaced.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Nesting does not help]
\label{rem:nesting}
Conditioning---``state $a$ makes $b$ probable, $b$ makes $c$ probable''---cannot
make a small target probable. The conditional probability of entering a macrostate
$X$ from any predecessor is bounded by the share of the predecessor's onward
measure that lands in $X$, and a small $X$ catches a small share by counting:
there is no predecessor from which most onward orbits enter a region that has few
states. The nested chain therefore drives occupation toward larger macrostates
(higher entropy), never toward smaller ones; it is the engine of the arrow, not a
route around it.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[The three errors share one form]
\label{thm:one-form}
Errors A, B, and C are instances of a single illegitimate transfer: a property
established for the agent's relation to a state (specification: naming, locating,
constructing) is carried onto the system's relation to the state (occupation), or
the constitutive measure of the state is silently altered. Under
\Cref{prin:asym} each transfer is blocked, because the two relations obey
opposite economies and the measure is identical to the probability.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Error A transfers a label (specification, mode~\ref{s:point}) onto a structural
predicate of occupation (having a past); blocked by \Cref{prop:err-rename}. Error
B transfers an agency claim (arrangement, mode~\ref{s:prepare}) onto a measure
fact (specificity); blocked by \Cref{prop:err-arrange}. Error C alters the
measure that \emph{is} the entropy (\Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob}); blocked by
\Cref{prop:err-prob}. In each case the move would require occupation to inherit
specification's cost-structure, or a state to change its measure while keeping its
identity; \Cref{prin:asym} and \Cref{rem:entropy-is-prob} forbid both.
\end{proof}

% =====================================================================
\section{Application: the Loschmidt reversal}
\label{sec:loschmidt}
% =====================================================================

We apply the framework to the Loschmidt reversal. We separate four claims that
the conventional discussion conflates, prove the three that are true, and locate
exactly the one that is neither true nor false but a category error.

\subsection{Four claims}

\begin{definition}[The reversal setting]
\label{def:reversal}
A time-reversal-symmetric flow $\phi_t$ on a bounded $\Space$ evolves $x_0$
forward to $x_\tau=\phi_\tau(x_0)$. The \emph{reversed state} is
$y_\tau=T(x_\tau)$, where $T$ negates momenta. Released, $y_\tau$ evolves forward
to $y_{2\tau}=\phi_\tau(y_\tau)$, whose configuration reflects $x_0$'s.
\end{definition}

The four claims are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(L\arabic*)},leftmargin=3em,nosep]
\item\label{l:identity} The reversed endpoint $y_{2\tau}$ \emph{is} the initial
state $x_0$.
\item\label{l:prepared} The reversal, when prepared by an agent, decreases total
entropy.
\item\label{l:incoherent} The spontaneous reversal (no agent) is logically
incoherent.
\item\label{l:value} On the reversed orbit, the thermodynamic entropy
$S=\kB\log\mu$ of the occupied macrostate decreases.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What is true}

\begin{theorem}[Non-identity]
\label{thm:non-identity}
\ref{l:identity} is false: $y_{2\tau}\neq x_0$. The reversed endpoint is a
distinct, later state that resembles a reflection of $x_0$ but is not $x_0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$y_{2\tau}=\phi_\tau(T(\phi_\tau(x_0)))$ is reached forward; it has the past
$[0,2\tau]$. The initial state $x_0$ is, in the structural sense, the origin of
the analysed window. By \Cref{prop:err-rename}, having a past, $y_{2\tau}$ is not
the no-past origin; at most it shares the configuration \emph{type} of a
reflection of $x_0$. Calling it ``initial'' is Error~A. Hence non-identity.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Prepared reversal pays]
\label{thm:prepared-pays}
\ref{l:prepared} holds only with compensation: an agent that prepares $y_\tau$
by negating $\sim\!N$ momenta conditional on the prior microstate dissipates
environmental entropy $\ge\Delta S$, the deficit it appears to create. No net
decrease results.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Direct application of \Cref{thm:landauer}: preparing the held-fixed
low-probability configuration $y_\tau$ requires acquiring and erasing the
information that specifies it, at Landauer cost $\ge\Delta S$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Spontaneous reversal is atypical, not incoherent]
\label{thm:atypical}
\ref{l:incoherent} is false. The spontaneous reversed orbit is a legal,
agent-free occupation event (\Cref{thm:rare}): it exists (recurrence), costs
nothing (no agent), and is merely rare (its macrostate has small measure).
Assuming its existence yields no contradiction---only an improbability of order
$p=\mu(X)/\mu(\Space)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{thm:rare}, the low-measure reflected macrostate is occupied infinitely
often by almost every orbit, with no agent and no cost. A contradiction would
follow from its existence only if existence entailed an impossible state of
affairs; it does not---the orbit is an ordinary forward trajectory of
$\phi_t$, distinct from the origin (\Cref{thm:non-identity}), depositing positive
individuation residue at each step it is \emph{analysed} (\Cref{thm:floor}) yet
itself costing nothing to \emph{occur} (\Cref{prop:occ-free}). The felt
incoherence is the cost of \emph{specifying} the orbit
(\Cref{prop:point-cost,thm:landauer}), transferred by Error~B/Error~C onto its
\emph{occurrence}. Removing the transfer, what remains is rarity, not
incoherence.
\end{proof}

\subsection{What is the case}

\begin{theorem}[The entropy value falls, rarely, uncompensated]
\label{thm:value}
\ref{l:value} is true and is not paradoxical. On the spontaneous reversed orbit
the occupied macrostate's measure decreases, so $S=\kB\log\mu$ falls; the fall is
uncompensated (no agent acts) and occurs with probability $\sim p(X)$. The second
law survives not because the fall is prevented or paid for, but because it is
atypical: the long-run fraction of time on entropy-decreasing segments is the
measure of the low-entropy region, $p(X)\ll 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The reflected orbit traverses macrostates of decreasing measure until it reaches
the reflected low-entropy configuration, whence (by Liouville volume
conservation, \citep{arnold1989}) it cannot decrease without bound and turns
upward. Along the decreasing segment $S=\kB\log\mu$ falls. By \Cref{thm:rare}(ii)
no agent compensates; by \Cref{thm:rare}(iii) the time fraction on such segments
is $p(X)$. The statistical second law---$S$ increases for the overwhelming
majority of orbits and time---is consistent with a measure-zero-in-frequency set
of decreasing segments.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The felt incoherence diagnosed]
\label{rem:diagnosis}
The reversal feels incoherent because every \emph{attempt to grasp it}---to point
to the reversed microstate, to prepare it, to recognise it---is genuinely costly
(\Cref{sec:spec}), and an embedded agent meets that cost at every turn. The cost
is real, and it is the content of the prepared-case resolution
(\Cref{thm:prepared-pays}). But the cost attaches to \emph{specification}, not to
\emph{occurrence}; transferred to occurrence it reads as impossibility. The
distinction of \Cref{prin:asym} is exactly the removal of that transfer: the
improbable event is costly to grasp and free to occur, and its survival of every
reframing is its irreducible rarity (\Cref{rem:bedrock}).
\end{remark}

% =====================================================================
\section{Scope and conclusion}
\label{sec:scope}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{What is established}

From the occupation--specification distinction and a bounded measure-preserving
setting we have established: that specification of a low-probability
configuration is irreducibly costly, and prepared specification pays at least its
apparent saving (\Cref{sec:spec}); that occupation of the same configuration is
free, agent-free, and scarce only in frequency (\Cref{sec:occ}); that the
standard moves against this are a single category error in three forms
(\Cref{sec:errors}); and that, applied to the Loschmidt reversal, the framework
proves non-identity, prepared-case compensation, and spontaneous-case
atypicality, locating the felt incoherence as the misattributed cost of
specification (\Cref{sec:loschmidt}).

\subsection{What is not claimed}

We do \emph{not} claim the spontaneous reversal is logically incoherent; the
evidence is that assuming it yields no contradiction, only improbability. We do
\emph{not} claim the boundary residue of \emph{analysing} the reversal transfers
to the \emph{thermodynamic} entropy of the system; the residue is agent-relative
and the entropy is system-relative, and the two are kept apart throughout. We do
\emph{not} derive the magnitude of the statistical arrow from the framework; that
magnitude is the measure ratio of macrostates, a datum about the state space and
its initial condition, not a consequence of the occupation--specification
distinction. Where the discussion reached for a logical impossibility, the result
delivered is a statistical atypicality, and we mark the difference rather than
elide it.

\subsection{Conclusion}

The improbable is costly to grasp and free to occur. To specify a
low-probability state---to name it, prepare it, recognise it---an embedded agent
must import order and pay a residue, and to prepare it deliberately is to pay at
least the entropy it seems to save. To occupy a low-probability state, a flow
need do nothing but wander, and it will, rarely. Confusing the cost of the first
for an impossibility of the second generates the appearance of paradox; keeping
them apart dissolves it. In the Loschmidt case what remains, after every
reframing is removed, is not an incoherence but a ratio: the low-entropy
configuration is real, uncompensated, unarranged, and outnumbered. Its rarity is
the whole of the arrow, and rarity is the one property no renaming, no nesting,
and no act of imagination can convert into something else.
